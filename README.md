**Investigating neuroreceptor-weighted fMRI signals across natural arousal state transitions**

*O Bajela, Harrison Fisher, Laura D Lewis*

Dopamine is a flexible neuromodulator that is involved in a diverse array of cognitive and behavioral functions such as mood, motivation, movement, learning, and arousal. Dopamine can be excitatory or inhibitory, depending on which of its two main receptor families it acts through (Costa & Schoenbaum, 2022). Dopamine historically was not considered one of the major neurotransmitter systems governing transitions between sleep and wakefulness; however, new evidence suggests that it may have a meaningful role in regulating arousal (Eban-Rothschild et al., 2018). 
           
Growing evidence suggests an important role in arousal control. Stimulants that target the dopaminergic system promote wakefulness, and sleep disturbances are common in conditions with disrupted dopamine function, such as Parkinson’s disease (Bollu & Sahota, 2017). Animal studies show striatal dopamine fluctuates across states, peaking in wakefulness and declining during REM sleep (Dong et al., 2019), though species differences limit translation to humans. Human imaging studies remain sparse and methodologically limited, with early SPECT findings (Lee et al., 2007) and recent work in animals (Eban-Rothschild et al., 2018) pointing to complex, projection specific dopaminergic contributions. Yet how dopamine and related neuromodulators dynamically modulate natural arousal transitions in healthy humans is largely unexplored.
   
To investigate these dynamics in humans, we can turn to non-invasive neuroimaging tools. Functional MRI (fMRI) provides a non-invasive method to monitor brain activity by measuring changes in oxygenated blood flow, which is influenced by neurovascular coupling. The neurovascular unit, which includes neurons, astrocytes, and vascular cells, manages blood flow in the brain based on neural activity (Iadecola, 2017). Neuromodulators like dopamine affect both neural firing and blood vessel tone and thus will influence the fMRI signal. My research aims to explore how the fMRI signal in dopamine-receptor rich areas changes with natural sleep and wake transitions. New tools like neuromaps (Markello et al., 2022) allow researchers to combine PET-derived neuroreceptor maps with fMRI data. These tools enable the comparison of brain activity patterns with receptor density distributions while accounting for spatial autocorrelation. However, their use in sleep studies has been limited. No prior research has used receptor-informed analysis to explore dopamine-sensitive fMRI signals during spontaneous transitions in arousal states in healthy individual
       
To address this gap, we analyzed a multimodal dataset of healthy adult participants who underwent simultaneous PET, fMRI, and EEG recordings. The scans were conducted in the early afternoon to take advantage of natural postprandial drowsiness. Participants lay quietly with eyes closed while performing a simple behavioral task to monitor responsiveness: pressing a button with each breath in and out. Changes in the rate of button pressing were used to identify three types of arousal transitions.  Loss of responsiveness events were defined as the last button press preceding ≥ 20s of no pressing.Sustained arousals were defined as the first press following ≥ 20 s of unresponsiveness that was followed by at least five consecutive responses. Transient arousals were defined as the first press following a ≥ 20s lapse that was followed by two or fewer consecutive responses. We investigated how fMRI signals changed around these three types of arousal transitions. We applied the neuromaps toolbox to extract neurotransmitter density-weighted fMRI time series, using PET-derived atlases of dopamine D1 and D2 receptors as well as norepinephrine transporter. Specifically, we computed weighted averages over the top 90% of voxels in each receptor atlas to maximize sensitivity while reducing noise. During preliminary analyses, we observed that global fMRI signal fluctuations strongly dominated these raw receptor-weighted timeseries. To address this, we implemented a sliding-window regression of the global gray matter signal, thereby removing broad fluctuations and isolating receptor-specific variance. After preprocessing, we aligned the receptor-weighted time series to behavioral arousal events defined from button pressing and extracted peri-event time courses for statistical modeling. At each timepoint in the peri-event windows, we fit linear mixed-effects models with receptor type and arousal category as fixed effects, and participant as a random effect. We assessed significance of the fixed effects by comparing nested models with and without the effect of interest using likelihood ratio tests. This statistical framework allowed us to account for within-subject variability and to test whether the observed signal changes reliably differed across receptor systems and behavioral states.
         
Our analyses revealed several preliminary findings. D2- and norepinephrine-weighted signals showed pronounced increases during behavioral arousals, whereas D1-weighted responses were weaker. Sustained arousals were associated with longer-lasting increases in receptor-weighted activity compared to transient arousals, which decayed rapidly. Loss-of-responsiveness events showed a brief receptor-weighted increase followed by a sharp decline, suggesting a transient neuromodulatory recruitment before disengagement. Likelihood ratio tests confirmed that both receptor type and arousal category significantly improved model fit. While these findings remain preliminary, they suggest that dopaminergic and noradrenergic activity may dynamically track arousal transitions in healthy humans.
               
Building on these findings, we expanded and refined our analyses in several directions. First, we strengthened statistical rigor by applying permutation-based clustering methods to control family-wise error across peri-event timepoints. This approach addresses the problem of multiple comparisons in our peri-event analyses and ensure that our effects reflect consistent temporal clusters rather than isolated fluctuations.
         
Our initial analyses revealed highly similar patterns of NE and D2 weighted fMRI signals across arousal transitions. We conducted further analyses to understand if these signals are inherently correlated, or if moments of arousal transitions organize similar activity changes across these regions. To disentangle these possibilities  we generated control events from random button presses and from presses distant from true arousals and repeat our analyses. This allowed us to test whether the observed dopaminergic and noradrenergic fluctuations are specific to genuine arousal transitions rather than respiratory cycle locked activity.

Dopamine D2 and norepinephrine density-weighted BOLD signals robustly increase during behavioral arousal transitions. These signal changes are more prolonged during sustained arousals compared to transient arousals. Loss of responsiveness is characterized by a subtle increase beginning a few seconds before the last button press, followed by a decrease in amplitude. No changes were observed around random button presses.
Linear mixed-effects modeling confirmed that arousal type and signal type both significantly affect fMRI signal amplitudes around arousal state transitions, with D2 and NE, but not D1, showing sensitivity to event type. The absence of D1 effects could reflect regional specificity or receptor affinity differences that are not detectable with the resolution of PET-derived weights.
         
In the longer term, we aim to integrate EEG-defined arousal markers as event triggers. EEG signals such as alpha rhythm suppression and delta power increases provide a well-established measure of sleep wake states. Incorporating these markers will enable direct comparison between behavioral and electrophysiological indices of arousal and receptor-informed fMRI signals. By combining behavioral, electrophysiological, and receptor-weighted measures, we will obtain a richer and more reliable characterization of neuromodulatory dynamics.
        
This work has several important implications. Clinically, understanding how dopaminergic and noradrenergic systems contribute to arousal regulation could inform new diagnostic or therapeutic approaches for conditions such as Parkinson’s disease, narcolepsy, or depression, in which both sleep and neuromodulation are disrupted. This work also advances the interpretation of the BOLD signal by integrating receptor distributions, helping to disentangle contributions of vascular, neural, and neuromodulatory processes. More broadly, this project demonstrates the feasibility of receptor-informed fMRI for studying neuromodulation in humans, offering a methodological framework that can be applied to other cognitive domains beyond arousal.<\body>
